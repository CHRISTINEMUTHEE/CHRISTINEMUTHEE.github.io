## The Question That Started It All

I had a quest. Not a career plan or a strategic pivot — a genuine, unsettled quest. A need to understand software engineering not just as a *tool*, the way I had learned it in Data Science, but as an *art*.

Why and how do people ship programs that are virtually error-free? How do Data Scientists move from the comfort of a Jupyter notebook to deploying production-ready code? What does it actually look like to work within a software engineering team — to have your code reviewed, your pull request merged, and to earn that satisfying blue checkmark on GitHub?

These questions had no easy answers. But they led me somewhere interesting.

---

## A Pivotal Read: When a Medium Article Changes Your Direction

Somewhere between these questions, I stumbled on an article by **Sara Nobrega**. Her argument was counterintuitive and immediately compelling: **Software Engineers, not Data Scientists, are more naturally positioned to step into ML/AI Engineering roles.**

Why? Because the core responsibilities of a software engineer are already aligned with what ML Engineering demands — taking experimental results and making them, and I quote, **"robust, scalable, and continuously deliverable."**

That phrase landed differently than I expected. I had spent years building models that performed beautifully in a notebook, only for them to crumble when they needed to scale, run on a schedule, or be understood by someone other than me.

It immediately raised two questions I could not ignore:

- What skills could I bring from my Data Science background?
- What would I need to deliberately add to my stack?

## What Surprised Me Most

The highlight — and the biggest surprise — was realising that **my Data Science skills were not obsolete in this world; they were indispensable in a different way.** My knowledge of data informed every decision about type safety, data handling, and model evaluation.

What was genuinely shocking was the *breadth* of engineering and design that goes into making Machine Learning production-ready. A performant model is only one small piece of the puzzle. Here is what completes the picture:

> **Unit tests** ensured that all anticipated errors were handled before they ever reached production.
>
> **Type checks** guaranteed a clean, predictable flow of the right data types throughout the pipeline.
>
> **System design** helped me prioritise maintainability and functionality over chasing short-term performance gains.
>
> **Logging configurations** dramatically reduced my debug time — because I finally knew exactly *what* went wrong and *where*.
>
> **Workflow scripts** collapsed the entire ML pipeline into a single command.

## A Note on Where I Landed

By the end of four weeks, something had clarified. **I love building tools just as much as I love discovering insights from data.** And as it turns out, those two passions are not as separate as I once thought. The best AI engineers I have encountered are not people who stopped being curious about data — they are people who became *equally* curious about the systems that serve that data.
